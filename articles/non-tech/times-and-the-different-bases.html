Times and the different bases

There are many different ways and styles to write time. Especially so when denoting the separation with the hour and minute digits.

22:10 vs 22.10 vs 2210
10:10pm in 24 hour format with 3 different common separators

Since young, I've been taught to use the colon(:) for all my hour-minute separation needs.
More recently however, I have started to see the dot/full-stop (.) being used more frequently in text to communicate time.

It is easier (on both phone and computer) to use the . over : when chatting. However, I've also started to see it more frequently in official announcements, publicity, etc.

While many can argue it's simply a style choice, I don't think it's as simple as that (When it is about relative timings - not absolute timings)

1. It is not linguistically accurate.

Text emulates speech (and vice versa). For example, "1:50" means "one hour, fifty minutes" with zero ambiguity. On the other hand. "1.50" either means "one point five hours" or "one hour, fifty minutes".

When someone says "It's one and a half hours", it can be represented in text as: "1:30, 1.5, 1.30". In this case, the decimal point has two correct representations: 1.5, and 1.30. These differing interpretations can conflict and lead to misunderstandings.

Of course, one can gather from contextual clues that 1.5 means "one and a half" and 1.50 means "one fifty". So honestly, it's not that bad.

2. They are different bases.

We use the '.' for many different reasons. Most notably, mathematics uses it as a decimal point for our base-10 system.

However, as it turns out, minutes are not base 100. An hour does not have 100 minutes, but 60. As such, minutes are functionally base-60.

As the full stop/decimal point is already used in mathematical contexts with a decimal base, applying the . makes less sense when referring to time; especially when there is :.
